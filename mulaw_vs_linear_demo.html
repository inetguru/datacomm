Why Laplace?
Speech amplitude is not uniform. Most of the time the signal is near silence, with occasional large bursts. The Laplace (double-exponential) distribution models this well:
f(x) = (1/2b)·exp(−|x|/b)
Linear quantization wastes levels on large amplitudes that rarely occur, giving poor SNR for quiet passages.
μ-Law Companding
Before quantization, the signal is compressed by the μ-Law function — expanding the quiet region and compressing the loud region. After quantization, the inverse is applied (expansion):
F(x) = sgn(x) · ln(1+μ|x|) / ln(1+μ)
This effectively allocates smaller steps near zero and larger steps at high amplitudes — matching where samples actually occur.
The Gain
μ-Law achieves nearly constant SQNR across all amplitude levels — the hallmark of good non-linear quantization. For voice (μ=255, k=8):
SQNR ≈ 6.02k − 7.2 dB
(roughly independent of amplitude)
This is why G.711 (telephone standard) uses 8-bit μ-Law instead of 12–13-bit linear PCM to achieve equivalent perceived quality.
